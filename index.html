fusion of texture

Not a fusion of pure frequencies or timbres, in the classic sense,

but a blending or melting of temporal features too:

fusion of texture
  • clock rates
  • “waves”
  • “peaks” yielding a sensation of Doppler shift (MiM)
  • periodicity of amplitude: e.g., implied metrical accents
fusion of texture

Once a periodicity between constituents has been established,

and when amplitude “peaks” fall out of sync,

or streams of “particles” are transposed into a new frequency range,

an emergent property quickly evaporates.

“thin” episodes

e.g., 0:10-0:31 — all 3 layers “refract” their material such that a fusion into the percept of rotational movement is temporarily lost.

“[...] the basic character of each sound is refracted due to a change in the temporal profile of the source, which obscures the original sensation of motion by altering the grain rate.” (74)

quality vs. form

“any quality (whose intensity can be defined through computation) can form the basis of form in music, in that its flux over time can be interpreted as form.

“[...] contrary to the assumption that there is a small
fixed set of quantitative dimensions [...]

“there are innumerable quantifiable qualities
whose flux of intensity bears form in music.”

—Joshua Banks Mailman, Temporal Dynamic Form in Music, vii.

vorticity / rotational movement
  • rotational movement is an emergent phenomenon
  • which represents some union between a texture and a timbre
  • Timbre is perceivably indivisible and quantitatively precise:
    i.e., we do not hear constituent frequencies apart from a whole
  • Texture is divisible but it is quantitatively imprecise:
    i.e., we do hear constituent “particles”
  • borderline states between texture/timbre: features may converge or cluster around a perceived quality, but may not be entirely measurable or divisible
